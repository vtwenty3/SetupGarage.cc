Setup Guides

First Things First

Understeer

Overly simplified - turning too little. This is when the front does not have enough grip, the tires start skidding and the car is simply not turning or turning far less than anticipated. This results in overheating the tires, missing the apex, or crashing. Normally, a car that tends to understeer is easier to drive for a beginner, but slower in a hands of experienced driver, as the understeer cannot be fixed with driving techniques. Front Engine cars tend to be more understeery.
Understeer Example: Imagine turning the wheel of the car on ice without pressing the gas or brake. The car just continues straight, without changing its direction. This is what understeer feels like.

Oversteer

Overly simplified - turning too much. Oversteer is when the front has grip but the rear loses traction on turning. The car is responsive, it turns, but it turns too much and you can easily lose control. Usually, oversteer cars are harder to drive for beginners. In oversttery car/setup can be faster on track than an understeery one for an experienced driver, as oversteer can be corrected with driving techniques, but understeer cannot. Usually, rear-wheel-drive cars oversteer on acceleration out of the corner.
Oversteer Example: Imagine a drift car, it is set up to oversteer, when turning it turns so sharply that the rear end kicks out and the front tires have enough grip to control the direction of the drift.

Corner Phases

Braking:
Pretty self-explanatory. Slowing down before the corner. For beginners having a stable and predictable car during the braking phase is really important as this helps place the car on the right position and prepare for the corner entry
Choose a breaking point, with the same car on the same conditions try to brake at the exact same spot each time. This would help you significantly in nailing the entry speed of the corner which is critical for getting faster.
Common beginner's mistake is heavy braking and turning at the same time.

Corner Entry:
At the beginning of the corner, at that stage, you should slowly come off the brakes and start gradually turning. At this stage oversteer is a common issue as all the weight of the car is transferred at the front of the car and the front tires are close to their limit of traction and the backend of the car is light and playful. With a bit too much speed you can end up understeering and missing the apex or oversteering and crashing into the wall. The oversteer, understeer depends on the car setup, the driving technique, and the elevation of the corner etc. Aim for a controlled corner entry. Everything depends on the car's position and the entry speed. The general rule is slow in fast out, which does not apply 100% of the situations but is overall faster technique. Most of the mistakes are choosing the correct speed for corner entry, but with good braking point as soon you start coming of the brakes you can turn and every time you would carry the same speed in the corner entry.

Mid Corner (apex):
At this point you should be off the brakes. At this stage you are doing most of the turning and the carâ€™s weight is distributed more evenly between the front and the back end. The general rule is in mid-corner not to break or touch the gas. Having a good setup would provide you with enough grip and stability to start pressing the throttle slightly approaching the corner exit, but this heavily depends on the car and the corner configuration.

Exit:
At this stage you should start decreasing the steering angle and applying throttle in syncron. Too much throttle and you will unsettle the car, understeer out of the grass or oversteer into the wall, too little and you will lose valuable lap time. The key is finding the perfect balance and timing. In general, it is wise to focus your driving on the corner exits.
The corner exit is the key to faster lap times as slow speed in the corner but solid, faster exit is giving you an advantage for the whole straight until the next corner as your car started accelerating before anyone else.

Camber and Toe


Toe influences 3 main things
Tyre Wear, Straight Line Stability, and Cornering. Regarding GT3 cars the usual setup is the negative toe on the front wheels and the negative toe on the rear wheels. Positive toe increase straight-line stability, negative toe improves turn-in response. High toe angle can result in rapid tyre wear and drag.
Camber
Camber influences tire wear, cornering ability, and straight-line speed. When cornering the car rolls sideways which results in changing of the angle of the tires touching the asphalt. The camber is used to counter that. The idea is to have a maximum surface area of the tire in the corner and less surface area on the straights. That gives less drag on the straights and more grip in the corners. A good camber setup would result in almost even temperatures of Inside and outside of the tire surface area. You can see that in the Tyre section in ACC near IMO.

Rake

Rake is the angle between the back and the front of the car. More rake means higher back-end and lower front end. A neutral rake is even front and back. The rake plays a big role in how the car behaves. More rake - more oversteer, less rake - more understeer. Rake changes the aero-balance of the car too.
